We data decisions.
We really, really do

It is true that protection data is extremely sensitive to be shared in its raw form. Equally true is the fact that we need to make programmatic decisions based on evidence. With this understanding, we have built tools using R, so that protection IM teams are able to extract non-person identiable data points using API calls and aggregate dataframes from different systems and analyze so that as a decision maker you don’t have to! Read more…

Most program managers still prefer to use Microsoft Excel to analyze data and make decisions. This is alright and is similar to driving down Mombasa Road in Nairobi and you see this guy with his horse-carriage competing with sleeky mercedes benz traffic. Didn’t someone tell him that horses have been decommissioned? To us in information management, Excel is the guy with his horses in a motorised transport environment. R is a mercedes, bentley or whatever sleek thing you drive these days. We deliberately use R for the following reasons:

  • Trends show how a community recalls the occurrence of protection concerns over time
  • Prevalence indicates how often such violations of these protection concerns happens
  • Most affected groups shows the most affected persons in the community
  • Connectors and dividers shows people who assist communities while dividers show spoilers

A plethora of data were acquired and analyzed to create the Intelligentsia model. Among those data sets are:

  • Retail environment data: Yelp ratings
  • Transportation data: NYC TLC taxicab trips, NYC Subway network and stations, residents’ travel time to work
  • Quality of life: Number and location of trees (NYC Open Data), Crime complaints (NYPD)
  • Online data: Edits of Wikipedia neighborhood articles, Google Search Trends
  • Housing: Median home value, gross rent, year structure built (Zillow, ACS/Census)
  • Demographics: Median income, age, education, poverty, employment (ACS/Census)
